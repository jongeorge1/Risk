Should I be taking Vitamin D supplements?

Scientists are still unsure about the role that vitamin D plays in prostate cancer. There are a couple of reasons why you might consider taking Vitamin D supplements:

Vitamin D may slow the progression of prostate cancer

Research in America suggests that taking Vitamin D supplements can slow down the cancer’s growth.

Vitamin D can help to prevent bone loss

Unfortunately hormone therapy can cause your bones to thin (osteoporosis), making you vulnerable to fractures. Before starting treatment with Zoladex, you should have had your Vitamin D levels measured because Vitamin D deficiency can make osteoporosis worse.

Your doctor will be able to tell you if your Vitamin D levels are low and advise you about taking supplements.

Will I be able to carry on exercising?

It all depends on the treatment you have had, how you feel and your general state of health. If you have had surgery, it may be a while before you are able to get back to your normal exercise routine. For the first few weeks you will need to be careful, it is probably best just to do some gentle walking. This will also help to prevent any blood clots forming in your legs. Once your wound has healed and you begin to feel stronger, you can gradually increase the amount of exercise each day. Your doctor will be able to advise you on the type of exercises that will be suitable.

If you have had radiotherapy, you may feel more tired than normal and a bit sore. If you feel up to it there is no reason why you shouldn’t start exercising again straight away, but start gradually and work up.

You may find that your bladder control is not as good as it used to be before treatment. Jumping, running or coughing may make you leak urine - this is called stress incontinence. Doing pelvic floor exercises regularly can help.

Although exercising may seem more of an effort, it is worth persevering because exercise:

  • Helps to keep weight under control - men who are obese have an increased risk of death from prostate cancer.
  • Helps to improve self-esteem - which can be lowered by side-¬-effects of treatment
  • Helps to lift mood - due to the release of endorphins Before starting a new exercise programme it is always best to check with your doctor first.

Will my partner get cancer if we have sex?

Prostate cancer cannot be transmitted through having sex. This applies equally to straight sex as to gay sex.

Any cells transferred from your body whether they are cancerous or not will be killed by your partner’s immune system.

How do I do pelvis floor exercises?

Your pelvic floor muscles are slung from your pubic bone to the end tip of your backbone. Their job is to keep everything in place. Your urethra (the tube carrying your urine away from the bladder) and your anus (the opening from your bowel) both pass through the pelvic floor muscles. If your pelvic floor muscles are weak you may find it difficult to control your flow of urine.

Finding your pelvic floor muscles

Before starting pelvic floor exercises you need to try flexing them so that you know where they are to make sure that you are exercising the right bits.

First imagine that you need to stop yourself passing wind (farting). The muscles you tighten to do this are the pelvic floor muscles around your anus.

Next tighten the muscles you would use to stop yourself weeing in mid flow. These are the pelvic floor muscles around your urethra.

A few pelvic floor exercises to start with

Now that you knowwhere they are, you can start to exercise your pelvic floor muscles. These exercises can be done sitting, standing or lying down. The more often you do them, the stronger your pelvic muscles will become and the more control you will have over your bladder and bowel. They can be done anytime, anywhere, whilst watching the TV, doing the washing up or first thing before you get out of bed in the morning.

  1. Pull up and hold - tighten your pelvic floor muscles right from your anus to your urethra as hard as you can and hold for a count of 5. Relax. Do this as many times as you can, gradually increasing the number every day.
  2. Fast pull up - this time tighten your muscles as quickly as you can as many times as you can.
  3. The lift – imagine you are using your muscles to go up in a lift. Tighten your muscles in stages, stopping at each floor (perhaps 5 in all) until you can’t tighten them any more. Gradually relax them again stopping at each floor on the way down.

If you are still having problems, ask your doctor to refer you to a physiotherapist who will be able to give you more pelvic floor exercise to try.

I will be away for my next hormone injection – what should I do?

It is very important to have your hormone injection at the right time, so that your testosterone levels stay low.

You should make every effort to plan holidays and trips around your injection appointments. If you already have something booked that clashes with your next injection appointment, you should speak to your GP or practice nurse as soon as you can.

It may be possible for them to give you your next injection before you go away or wait until you get back.

Symptoms and Side Effects

What symptoms will I notice if my cancer has spread?

Here are some of the things that might be due to prostate cancer spread:

  • Low back pain
  • Bone pain in other parts of your body
  • Not being able to pass urine or stools
  • Swelling in the lower legs
  • Lethargy and severe tiredness
  • Weight loss

It’s important to remember that these symptoms can also be caused by many other conditions. So you shouldn’t assume they are necessarily due to prostate cancer.

Sometimes worrying about the cancer spreading might make you feel like you are starting to get these symptoms, when you are not. Or you may have them, but blank them out, not wanting to acknowledge them.

You need not feel alone with these worries. Your care team are there to support you and there are other charities and support organisations to help you.

I get very embarrassed when I get a hot flush – what can I do?

Hot flushes are a common side-effect of hormonal therapy. They vary in severity. Some men just feel a bit hot or prickly; others go red in the face and sweat profusely.

If you suffer from hot flushes, you may feel very self-conscious about it. You may think it is very obvious to other people and that they are staring at you. In reality, other people usually aren’t that interested and probably haven’t noticed. Think back yourself to how many times you’ve noticed a woman of menopausal age having a hot flush.

If hot flushes and sweating bother you, there are things that you can do to lessen the effects including:

  • Cutting down on alcohol, caffeine and spicy food.
  • Keeping the room cool
  • Using cotton sheets and clothing
  • Dressing in layers which can quickly be removed

If your symptoms are very severe, it might be worth asking your doctor about treatments that may help you.

Some men find that complementary therapies such as auricular acupuncture can help to reduce the frequency and intensity of hot flushes.

I often need to go to the toilet urgently - how can I make sure I’m at the front of the queue?

Needing to go to the bathroom more often or more urgently is a common complaint for men with prostate cancer. For some men, it can become such a big issue that they dread going out in case they are suddenly caught short.

It is important not to let your urinary problems rule your life. You still need to go out for your own sanity as much as for anything else. There are things you can do to help:

  • Ask your doctor to refer you to a continence advisor.
  • Wear incontinence pads or pants if you need to go out, just in case.
  • If possible avoid drinking for a couple of hours before you go out.
  • Make sure you know where the public toilets are.
  • Find out which shops have loos that you can use.
  • Obtain a Just can’t wait card. The card states that you have a medical condition that means you require the urgent need of a toilet. You can use it to show to shopkeepers or in other situations when you need a toilet without having the embarrassment of having to ask if you can use their toilet and explaining why. You can also use it to show to people if there is a long queue for the only toilet around. To find out how to get a just can’t wait card go to: www.bladderandbowelfoundation.org

I can feel a lump where the hormone is injected – is this normal?

You may be able to sometimes feel the injected fluid or implant under your skin, this is perfectly normal.

Over time the fluid or implant gradually dissolves, releasing the active drug into your body.

I don’t like injections – can I have hormone tablets instead?

No. Hormone analogues are currently only available as injections.

The injection has been specially formulated to release the active ingredient at a steady rate, to keep testosterone levels low throughout the day.

Another benefit is that you don’t have to remember to take tablets every day and the risk of forgetting to take your medicine is removed.

Most men find that the injection is not very painful and they only need to be given every 4 or 12 weeks.

I feel well, but sex has become a real problem – can I stop my hormone injections?

Hormonal treatments can lower your sex drive and make it more difficult to get or keep an erection. However there are things that you can do to make things better.

Please speak to your doctor or nurse about currently available therapies. It is important not to stop your hormone injections unless you are instructed to do so by your healthcare professional.

Tests and Investigations

I feel very uncomfortable having rectal examinations. Can I avoid these, or make them less embarrassing?

The digital rectal examination is a good way for doctors to assess your prostate. When you were first diagnosed you will have had this test and may not need it again. If the doctor decides that they need to feel your prostate again, or do an ultrasound, you can ask why. Knowing why this test is important for you, and how it can influence decisions, might make a few moments of indignity seem more worthwhile.

Here are some ideas to help get over embarrassment:

  • Say how embarrassed you feel.
  • Make a joke about the situation.
  • Pretend its not happening to you.
  • Imagine you are somehwere else.
  • Don't think about what is going on.
  • Ask the doctor to talk you
    through what's going on.

I’ve had my prostate removed, but my PSA is still high - what does this mean?

The idea of prostate removal (radical prostatectomy) is to remove all the cancer cells so they can’t spread round the body. With no prostate to make PSA, levels should fall. Radiation therapy has similar aims.

If the PSA level does not fall, or continues to rise, this can mean the cancer is still in the ‘prostate bed’ (the area around the prostate) or that it had already spread before the surgery or radiotherapy.

Sometimes doctors look at PSA doubling time to predict spread of disease. A PSA doubling time of less than 3 months is associated with more serious disease.

This can be a disappointing outcome, but there are still many treatment options that your specialist can consider. The choice will depend on whether the cancer remains in, or near the prostate, or whether it has spread.

I don’t know my Gleason score/prostate stage – how can I find out?

Men who have confirmed prostate cancer will have a Gleason score and their cancer will have been ‘staged’.

Because these two aspects of diagnosis are important in making treatment decisions you will have probably been told what they are. They may even have been written down for you. But when there’s a lot to take in it’s easy to forget what’s been said – or lose notes or papers.

If you think it would be helpful to have these figures, you can ask for them again. Your hospital doctor or clinical nurse specialist should be able to help. Your GP may also have this information, although it could take a little longer to reach your records.

Discussing your Cancer with Others

I’m not sure of the right words to use when I’m talking to my doctor – does it matter?

Lots of men with prostate cancer today went to school in the 1940s or ‘50s before science was taught like it is today. You might not have a very good understanding of how your body works, or all the correct terms. You may feel uneasy using medical words, or worry that you’ve made a mistake.

  • It’s fine to use the terms and words you do understand and feel comfortable with
  • You can look up terms you don’t understand
  • Don’t be afraid to ask a doctor or nurse if you want something clarified
  • You may feel embarrassed, but healthcare professionals who work with men with prostate cancer are used to helping men who are feeling awkward
  • If you’re feeling uncomfortable, or unable to explain, it is fine to say so

I have trouble remembering the things my doctor tells me – is it ok to write notes?

Lots of people have trouble remembering what their doctor tells them. When you have cancer, discussions can seem complicated. You might also feel emotional, or have received news that you need time to get used to. Plus we can all get a little more forgetful as we get older.

In any consultation it is fine to take along notes to help you remember important questions. It is also OK to take notes of what’s been said, or ask your healthcare professional to jot down things down for you.

You might worry that this is a bit impolite, but your care team want you to have the information you need about your health, so you can take part in the decisions that affect you.

Do I have to tell me boss – he/she might sack me?

You do not have to tell your employer that you have been diagnosed with prostate cancer. However there are several reasons why it is a good idea to do so:

  • It will make it easier to ask for time off work if you need to go to a hospital appointment or have to have more treatment.
  • Your employer may be able to make adjustments to your job or working conditions to make the job less tiring and allow you to carry on working for longer.
  • It will allow your employer to make arrangements for others to cover you if you are off sick or need to work fewer hours.

Know your rights

You may be worried that your boss will try to get rid of you if he knows you have cancer. Fortunately these days you are protected by employment law. If you feel that you are being discriminated against in any way because of your prostate cancer, you can get advice on what to do from your local Citizens Advice Bureau. To find your local branch visit www.citizensadvice.org.uk

I’ve already got annual travel insurance – do I need to tell them about my diagnosis?

Most annual travel insurance policies require you to tell them if you are diagnosed with a new condition such as prostate cancer. They may also want to know about any tests, treatments or changes to medication that you may have.

If you do not tell them, it could invalidate your policy and they may not pay any claims you make. If you do make a claim, the insurance company may write to your GP to get your medical information, so it pays to be honest.

It is always best to read your insurance policy carefully and provide the insurance company with correct, up-to-date and complete information about your medical condition.

I have prostate cancer – should my male relatives be screened?

Because prostate cancer can run in families your sons and brothers are at higher risk of prostate cancer. Because of this increased risk male relatives might want to consider prostate cancer screening. This is something that each relative should discuss with their own GP.

Will I have to pay for my prescriptions?

No. From April 2009 the government abolished prescription charges for everyone undergoing treatment for:

  • Cancer
  • The effects of cancer
  • The effects of treatments of cancer

To get your free prescription you will need to:

  • Fill in an application form (form FP92A) to obtain a 5-year exemption certificate for prescription charges – this form should be available from your GP or cancer clinic.
  • Ask your GP or hospital doctor to countersign the form
  • Send the completed form to the address given

If after 5 years you still need treatment, you can renew your exemption certificate.
